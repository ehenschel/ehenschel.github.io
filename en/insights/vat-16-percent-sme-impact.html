How the VAT Increase to 16% Impacts SMEs: What Your Business Needs to Know Now

Kazakhstan's standard VAT rate jumped from 12% to 16% on January 1, 2026 — the first increase in over a decade. For small and medium enterprises, the effects extend far beyond a line item on an invoice.

The Direct Cost: 4 Percentage Points

For businesses that are already VAT-registered, the arithmetic is straightforward but the impact is not. A product that previously carried KZT 12 in VAT per KZT 100 of net price now carries KZT 16. The question every business owner is facing: who absorbs the difference?

If you pass the increase through to your customers, your prices rise by approximately 3.6% (the difference between a 12% and 16% VAT-inclusive calculation). In sectors with elastic demand — retail, food service, discretionary consumer goods — this can translate into reduced sales volume. If you absorb the increase by holding prices steady, your effective margin shrinks by the full 4 percentage points of additional VAT on your output.

In practice, most businesses will land somewhere in between: partially passing through the cost, partially absorbing it. The key is to model both scenarios for your specific margin structure before making pricing decisions.

The Threshold Drop: More Businesses, More Compliance

Perhaps more consequential than the rate increase is the reduction of the mandatory VAT registration threshold from 20,000 MCI to 10,000 MCI. Under the new Code, any business with annual turnover exceeding approximately KZT 43.25 million (roughly USD 90,000) must register for VAT.

Under the previous threshold of approximately KZT 73 million, a significant number of small businesses operated entirely outside the VAT system. They neither charged VAT nor reclaimed it. The lower threshold pulls these businesses into full VAT compliance: charging 16% on their output, maintaining electronic invoicing records, filing VAT returns, and navigating the new e-Tamga prepayment system.

Who is newly affected: Businesses with annual turnover between KZT 43.25 million and KZT 73 million are now required to register for VAT for the first time. This bracket captures a substantial portion of Kazakhstan's SME sector — from professional service providers and trade businesses to mid-sized retail operations.

The e-Tamga System: VAT Before You Invoice

The new Code introduces e-Tamga, an electronic VAT account system that represents a fundamental change in how VAT is administered. Under this system, businesses must deposit VAT funds into a dedicated tax account before they can issue an electronic invoice (ESF). The system is designed to prevent VAT fraud, but its practical effect on cash flow is immediate.

For SMEs, this means that VAT is no longer simply a line item that flows through your accounting at the end of the reporting period. It becomes a real-time cash requirement tied to each transaction. A business issuing a KZT 10 million invoice must have KZT 1.6 million available in its e-Tamga account before the invoice can be generated. This is a working capital discipline that many small businesses have not previously faced.

Cash flow planning required

Businesses should model their monthly invoicing volume and ensure sufficient e-Tamga account balances to avoid delays in invoice issuance. A delayed invoice means a delayed ability to charge VAT, which in turn can disrupt your entire billing cycle.

Sector-Specific Effects

Real estate

Residential real estate sales, previously VAT-exempt, are now subject to the standard 16% rate. For developers and property companies, this changes the entire pricing calculus for residential projects. Buyers will see the VAT component reflected in the final price, and developers must decide how to structure their pricing — and their input VAT recovery — accordingly.

Healthcare and pharmaceuticals

The sale of medicines, medical devices, and medical supplies moves from full exemption to a reduced rate of 5% in 2026, increasing to 10% from 2027. Paid medical services are taxed at 10%. While the rates are lower than the standard 16%, this still represents a new cost for a sector that was previously untouched by VAT.

Digital services from abroad

Foreign companies selling digital services to Kazakh consumers must now register for VAT in Kazakhstan and charge 16% on their services. A registry of foreign VAT payers is being established. For domestic businesses that compete with foreign digital service providers, this levels the playing field — those competitors can no longer undercut on price by omitting VAT.

The Simplified Regime Incompatibility

Under the new Code, businesses operating under the simplified declaration regime (the 4% combined rate) cannot simultaneously be registered as VAT payers. This creates a forced choice for any SME whose turnover crosses the 10,000 MCI threshold: stay on the simplified regime and accept that you cannot charge or recover VAT, or move to the general regime with full VAT compliance and a 20% CIT rate.

For businesses with significant input VAT (those that purchase materials, equipment, or services from VAT-registered suppliers), the general regime may actually result in a lower effective tax burden despite the higher headline CIT rate — because input VAT recovery can offset a substantial portion of the output VAT. The calculation is business-specific, and getting it wrong locks you into the chosen regime for the year.

What to Do Now

If your business is affected by the VAT changes — whether through the rate increase, the threshold reduction, or the regime incompatibility — three steps are immediately relevant. First, review your annual turnover against the new 10,000 MCI threshold and determine whether you are now required to register. Second, model your pricing under the 16% rate to determine how much to pass through versus absorb. Third, assess your e-Tamga cash flow requirements and build VAT prepayment into your working capital planning.
