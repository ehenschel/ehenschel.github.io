Kazakhstan's New Tax Code 2026: A Comprehensive Analysis for Business

On July 18, 2025, President Tokayev signed into law the most sweeping fiscal reform since Kazakhstan's independence. The new Tax Code, effective January 1, 2026, reshapes the entire tax landscape. Here is what your business needs to understand.

February 15, 2026 · 12 min read · MinTax Group Tax Advisory

Context: Why a New Code, Not an Amendment

Kazakhstan's previous Tax Code, enacted in December 2017, had undergone hundreds of individual amendments over seven years. By 2024, its internal logic had become fragmented: overlapping special regimes, inconsistent benefit structures, and administrative procedures that no longer reflected how businesses actually operated. The government's response was not incremental reform but a complete rewrite.

The new Code (Law No. 214-VIII ZRK, dated July 18, 2025) replaces the 2017 Code in its entirety. It reduces the total number of taxes by 20%, cuts tax reporting volume by 30%, and consolidates seven special tax regimes into three. The stated objectives are simplification, digitalization, and broadening the tax base to reduce dependence on extractive-sector revenue.

Key figure: The MCI (Monthly Calculation Index) for 2026 is set at KZT 4,325. This figure underpins every threshold, penalty, and benefit limit referenced in this analysis.

VAT: From 12% to 16%

The most immediately felt change is the increase in the standard VAT rate from 12% to 16%. This is Kazakhstan's first VAT rate adjustment in over a decade and brings the country closer to the OECD average of approximately 19%.

What changed

Parameter Previous Code New Code (2026)
Standard VAT rate 12% 16%
VAT registration threshold 20,000 MCI (~KZT 73M) 10,000 MCI (~KZT 43.25M)
Medical services / pharmaceuticals Exempt 5% (2026), 10% (2027+)
Residential real estate sales Exempt Subject to VAT
Foreign e-commerce VAT Limited 16% on digital services, mandatory registry

The lower registration threshold means that businesses with annual turnover exceeding approximately KZT 43.25 million (roughly USD 90,000) must now register for VAT. This pulls a significant number of small and medium enterprises into the VAT system for the first time.

The e-Tamga system

Alongside the rate increase, the Code introduces the e-Tamga electronic VAT account system. Under this mechanism, businesses must deposit VAT funds into a dedicated tax account before they can issue electronic invoices (ESF). The system is designed to close VAT fraud schemes where invoices were issued without corresponding tax remittance. In practice, this means working capital planning must now account for pre-payment of VAT at the transactional level.

Corporate Income Tax: Differentiated Rates

The base CIT rate remains at 20% for most enterprises. However, the new Code introduces a differentiated structure for specific sectors:

Category CIT Rate Notes
Standard enterprises 20% Unchanged
Banks (non-lending income) 25% Lending income to business entities remains at 20%
Gambling businesses 25% New elevated rate
Social sector organizations 5% (2026), 10% (2027+) Transitional support
Agricultural producers 3% Preferential rate maintained
Newly developed low-profit deposits 0% (5 years) Investment incentive for subsoil use

Of particular note for the banking sector: the 25% rate applies only to income not derived from lending to business entities. Banks that focus on corporate lending will continue at the 20% effective rate on that portion of income. However, income from securities, interbank transactions, and other non-lending activities will be taxed at the elevated rate.

Personal Income Tax: Progressive Rates

Kazakhstan has moved from a flat 10% PIT rate to a progressive structure. This is a fundamental shift in personal tax philosophy, introducing income-dependent taxation for the first time.

Income Type Threshold Rate
Wages Up to 8,500 MCI (~KZT 36.8M/year) 10%
Wages Above 8,500 MCI 15%
Dividends Up to 230,000 MCI (~KZT 995M) 5%
Dividends Above 230,000 MCI 15%

For the vast majority of wage earners, nothing changes: the 10% rate applies to annual income up to approximately KZT 36.8 million (roughly USD 75,000). The 15% bracket targets high-income earners and large dividend recipients. Employers must update their payroll systems to calculate the marginal rate correctly when compensation crosses the threshold.

Social Payments: Restructured, Not Reduced

The social payment structure has been reorganized in a way that appears to shift the balance between employer contributions and social tax. Mandatory employer pension contributions increase from 2.5% to 3.5%, while social tax is reduced from 11% to 6%. However, the right to reduce the social tax base by the amount of social deductions has been eliminated, effectively preserving the total employer burden at roughly the same level.

Practical impact: While the headline rates look favorable (lower social tax), the removal of the deduction offset means that the total cost of employment has not meaningfully decreased. Employers should recalculate their fully-loaded labor costs under the new formula before making workforce planning decisions.

Special Tax Regimes: Seven to Three

The consolidation of special tax regimes represents the Code's most structurally significant change. The previous seven regimes — patent, simplified declaration, special mobile application, retail tax, and others — have been reduced to three:

1. Self-employed regime. Designed for individuals with monthly income up to 340 MCI (approximately KZT 1.47 million). Only social payments are due; no individual income tax. All reporting and receipt issuance is handled through a dedicated mobile application.

2. Simplified declaration. The primary regime for small and medium business. Annual income limit raised to 600,000 MCI (approximately KZT 2.6 billion). The combined CIT/IIT rate is 4%, with local maslikhats empowered to adjust this by up to 50% in either direction. No social tax is due. No limit on the number of employees. Businesses may operate branches. Crucially, under the new Code the simplified declaration regime cannot be combined with VAT payer status — this is a strategic decision every business must evaluate.

3. Peasant and farm households. The former unified land tax has been replaced with a 0.5% IIT rate, payable annually. No VAT obligation except on imports.

Critical Deadline: March 1, 2026

Every taxpayer who operated under a special tax regime in 2025 must submit a notification to the tax authority confirming their chosen regime by March 1, 2026. Failure to submit results in automatic deregistration as an individual entrepreneur, retroactive to January 1, 2026. This is not a grace period — it is an administrative cutoff with immediate legal consequences.

International Taxation

Several provisions affecting cross-border operations deserve attention. The definition of royalties has been expanded to explicitly include payments for software version updates — a provision that affects virtually every company with foreign SaaS subscriptions or software licensing agreements. Bug fixes and error corrections are specifically excluded from the royalty definition.

The recognition period for unfulfilled advances to non-residents has been shortened from two years to twelve months. If a foreign supplier fails to deliver goods or services within one year, the advance payment is automatically reclassified as taxable income. This change tightens the window for managing cross-border prepayment arrangements.

For technology companies operating within Astana Hub, the tax exemption on payments to non-residents for income and royalties has been extended until 2029 — a welcome signal for the IT sector.

Enforcement and Administration

The new Code introduces two notable enforcement mechanisms. First, a temporary travel ban may be imposed on executives of companies with tax debts exceeding a specified threshold that remain unpaid for more than three months. Second, the suspension of electronic VAT invoice issuance has been introduced as a formal enforcement measure, effectively preventing non-compliant taxpayers from participating in the VAT chain.

On the administrative side, the shift toward digital-first operations is comprehensive. All tax reporting must now be submitted online. Desk audits will be conducted automatically. Where a taxpayer fails to file, zero-value declarations will be generated automatically by the system. The existing risk management system (SUR) has been replaced by a new tax risk management system (SUNR).

Limitation periods for tax offences remain at three years for most taxpayers but have been extended to five years for large businesses, subsoil users, and companies benefiting from investment incentives.

What This Means: Strategic Implications

The new Tax Code is not simply a collection of rate adjustments. It represents a deliberate reorientation of Kazakhstan's fiscal architecture toward broader tax base coverage, reduced dependence on extractive industries, and digital administration. For businesses operating in Kazakhstan, the practical implications are substantial.

VAT planning requires immediate attention. The combination of a higher rate, a lower registration threshold, and the e-Tamga pre-payment system means that working capital management, pricing strategy, and supplier negotiations all need to be reassessed. Companies that were previously below the registration threshold may now be required to charge and remit VAT for the first time.

Regime selection is a once-a-year decision with annual consequences. The incompatibility of the simplified declaration regime with VAT payer status forces a clear choice: remain on the simplified regime (no VAT, 4% flat) or move to the general regime (full VAT compliance, standard CIT, but input VAT recovery). There is no middle ground.

Payroll recalculation is mandatory. The combination of progressive PIT, restructured social payments, and new pension contribution rates means every employer's payroll system must be updated. The apparent headline reduction in social tax is offset by the removal of base deductions.

Cross-border operations should be reviewed. The expanded royalty definition, the shortened advance recognition window, and the foreign e-commerce VAT registry all have implications for companies with international supply chains or digital service providers.
